# SECTION 1: HOOKS

## The Hook Job
Your hook has ONE job: Make them stop scrolling. If they don't stop, nothing else matters.

### SaaS-Specific Psychology
This audience is:
- **Skeptical of hype** — they've seen too many "10x your growth" posts
- **Data-driven** — they trust numbers over claims
- **Time-poor** — get to the point in line 1
- **Secretly emotional** — they care about shipping, building, winning
- **Community-minded** — "we're all in this together" energy works

### What Triggers SaaS Readers
- Metrics they recognise (ARR, churn, NPS, CAC, LTV, DAU)
- Situations they've lived (failed launch, investor pressure, feature bloat)
- Contrarian takes on tools and "best practices"
- Founder-to-founder honesty corporate blogs never publish
- Speed — "we did X in 30 days" beats "after years of learning"

### Hard Rules for This Niche
- NEVER start with "As a founder/PM/engineer..." — overused, skippable
- NEVER use "leveraged" or "utilized" — sounds like a press release
- NEVER write hooks that could apply to any industry — be specific to SaaS
- ALWAYS tie to measurable outcome (ARR, churn %, conversion rate)
- ALWAYS match language to seniority — founder voice ≠ developer voice

## HOOK TEMPLATES

### Template 1: "Your [X] is broken — and it's not your fault"
Making readers feel seen while shifting blame from them to the system.

```
Your [churn/growth/retention/onboarding] is broken — and it's not your fault.

[VC playbooks / YC advice / growth frameworks] weren't built for your stage.

Here's what actually works at [specific stage/ARR]:
```

**Real Example:**
```
Your churn isn't a product problem.

It's a psychology problem.

Your users aren't leaving because your feature set is weak.
They're leaving because you designed onboarding for people who already understood your product — not humans who need to get it in 30 seconds.

Here's what that looks like:
```

### Template 2: "Stop This"
Calling out a specific bad practice with a reason why it fails.

```
Never start your [product roadmap / sprint / OKRs] with [common bad practice].

You're not [Stripe/Notion/Figma]. And [reason this works at scale kills early-stage]:
```

**Real Example:**
```
Never build a feature because a big customer asked for it.

You're not their product team. And one loud enterprise voice will steer your roadmap off a cliff.
```

### Template 3: "You're Not Fooling Anyone"
Calling out cargo cult behavior.

```
Are you [doing the SaaS equivalent of cargo cult behaviour]?

Just [copying what successful companies did at scale] and hoping [it applies to your stage]?
```

**Real Example:**
```
Are you building a second product because the first one is hard to grow?

Just adding features instead of solving the distribution problem?
```

---

### Template 4: "Not What's Stopping You"
Flipping a common belief about what's blocking progress.

```
Most [SaaS founders / PMs / growth teams] know exactly [common knowledge].

The problem isn't [what they blame].
```

**Real Example:**
```
Most SaaS founders know how to reduce churn.

The problem isn't knowledge. It's that fixing churn means admitting the product isn't sticky enough.

And nobody wants to have that meeting.
```

---

### Template 5: "Costly Mistakes"
Specific numbers, honest vulnerability.

```
We made [X] mistakes in our first [Y] months.

([Specific cost in $, ARR, churn, months wasted])
```

**Real Example:**
```
We burned $180k and 9 months building a feature nobody asked for.

Not because we were stupid. Because we asked "can we build this?" instead of "should we?"
```

### Template 6: "The Rising Trend (That's a Problem)"
Calling out a hot trend with specific mockery.

```

```

**Real Example:**
```
There's a growing trend in SaaS to add AI to the product, call it "AI-powered," put it on the homepage, and call it a feature.

Meanwhile the core product still has a 60% drop-off at step 3 of onboarding.
```

## RAGE BAIT HOOKS (For SaaS)

### Type 1: "The Sacred Tool Kill"
```
[Beloved tool or framework] is [overrated / broken / costing you].

[One line that hints at why without giving it away]
```

**Example:**
```
OKRs are making your team worse, not better.

Not because goal-setting is bad. Because most teams use OKRs to justify what they were already going to do.
```

### Type 2: "Unpopular Opinion"
```
Unpopular opinion: [Widely held SaaS belief you can defend disagreeing with]
```

**Example:**
```
Unpopular opinion: Most early-stage SaaS companies should not have a product roadmap.

A roadmap is a commitment to building things before you know what customers actually need.
```

## BAD HOOK vs GOOD HOOK

### ❌ BAD:
```
Most SaaS founders know how to grow their business.
The problem is they don't take enough action.
```

**Why it fails:** Preachy, vague, condescending. This audience hates being lectured.

### ✅ GOOD:
```
Most SaaS founders know exactly what their churn problem is.

The problem isn't knowledge. It's that fixing churn means admitting the product isn't sticky enough.

And nobody wants to have that meeting.
```

**Why it works:** Emotionally accurate, names the real blocker (ego/fear), creates recognition.

# SECTION 2: POSTS

## The Post Job
After the hook stops them, the post must KEEP them reading. Every paragraph should deepen the feeling from the hook. Don't explain the emotion — amplify it.

### SaaS Post Rules
- Use metrics whenever possible — this audience respects numbers
- Keep paragraphs to 1-2 sentences — they read fast
- Use numbered lists for steps/lessons, arrows (→) for outcomes
- Write at the level of a smart operator, not a thought leader
- Avoid jargon unless writing for a technical dev audience
- Endings: question ("what's your churn interview process?") or hard truth statement

### The Emotional Arc Formula

```
[Emotional Hook] → "your churn is broken"
        ↓
[Emotional Proof] → "here's what we found analyzing 2,400 companies"
        ↓
[Emotional Twist] → "it's not the product. it's the psychology."
        ↓
[Solution + Emotion] → "here's what actually works (and why)"
        ↓
[Action/CTA] → "what's your churn interview process?"
```

**The mistake:** Jumping to "here's the framework" before they've felt why they need it.

## POST TEMPLATES

### Template 1: "The Dumb Thing Everyone Does"
Teaching through observation of a widespread mistake.

```
[Common SaaS practice] is expensive and we don't talk about it enough.

[What teams do]

[The result they think they're getting]

[The real cost — in ARR, time, team morale, or churn]

[What I do instead — specific, actionable]

[measurable result]

Stop [the dumb thing].

```

```
Result: 40% improvement in unit economics.

Stop chasing enterprise features before you have product-market fit.
Your sanity will thank you.
```

### ❌ BAD:
```

Here are 5 tips:

2. Reduce churn
3. Improve onboarding
4. Raise prices
5. Build a great team
```

**Why it fails:** Generic, listy, obvious. Could be written by anyone. No specificity.

```

With three emails almost nobody reads.

We started sending them 7 days AFTER signup — when the "magic moment" was supposed to happen but usually didn't.

Here's the sequence:
```

**Why it works:** Specific numbers, counter-intuitive insight, actionable framework, real story.

# SECTION 3: LEAD MAGNETS

## The Lead Magnet Job
The goal is lead generation: driving comments, DMs, or downloads. BUT — if readers feel "this is selling" — they scroll past.

**The key:** Make the lead magnet feel like a GIFT, not a transaction.

### What SaaS Audiences Respond To
- Templates they can use immediately (actual templates, not "frameworks")
- Real data — benchmarks, cohort data, churn numbers, conversion rates
- Systems and processes — exact workflows, not general principles
- Swipe files — copy, emails, onboarding sequences that worked
- Calculators — LTV, CAC payback, churn impact

### Investment Signaling (Instead of "Download My Free Guide")
```
❌ BAD: "Download my free guide to better onboarding"
✅ GOOD: "I spent 27 hours creating this framework after analyzing 2,400 companies"
```

When you prove you've invested real time, readers think "if they spent 27 hours on this, it must be good. And they're giving it away free? I should check it out."

This triggers reciprocity without feeling salesy.

## LEAD MAGNET ANTI-PATTERNS

### ❌ BAD:
```
📥 FREE GUIDE: 27 Email Templates That Convert!

Get my free guide to better email marketing!

Comment " GUIDE" to get it! 🎁
```

**Why it fails:** Sounds like marketing. Generic. Everyone promises "free guides."

### ✅ GOOD:
```
We beat our competitors by writing one page of copy instead of 50.

The 50-page "complete guide" approach: everyone's doing it.
The 1-page "specific solution" approach: nobody's doing it well.

I've documented exactly what we wrote and why — the 1-page swipe file that changed our conversion rate by 40%.

Comment "SWIPE" — I'll send it over. No catch.
```

**Why it works:** Specific insight, challenges conventional approach, feels like sharing real knowledge, not hawking a product.

---

## CTA FORMATS THAT DON'T FEEL SALESY

| ❌ Salesy | ✅ Natural |
|----------|-----------|
| "Download my free guide!" | "I made this for myself. Then others asked for it — here's what we built." |
| "Limited time offer!" | "I'm testing this with a small group. Just comment and I'll send it." |
| "Sign up for my newsletter!" | "I write about this every week. If it's useful, follow along." |
| "Book a call with me!" | "If this resonated, tell me in the comments. I read every one." |

---

> **Key Principle:** The best lead magnets don't ask. They give first, then invite. If the reader feels "this is helping me" before you ask for anything, they'll happily give you their contact.
