# LinkedIn Hook Templates — SaaS & Tech

## SKILL OVERVIEW
Use this file when writing the **opening hook** for LinkedIn posts in the **SaaS or tech niche**.
This includes: SaaS founders, product managers, developers, growth marketers at tech companies, CTOs, and tech operators.

---

## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — Know your SaaS/Tech audience's psychology
This audience is:
- **Skeptical of hype** — they've seen too many "10x your growth" posts
- **Data-driven** — they trust numbers over claims
- **Time-poor** — get to the point in line 1
- **Secretly emotional** — they care about shipping, building, winning, and being right
- **Community-minded** — they like "we're all in this together" energy

### Step 2 — What triggers SaaS/Tech readers specifically
- Metrics they recognise (ARR, churn, NPS, CAC, LTV, DAU)
- Situations they've lived (failed launch, investor pressure, feature bloat)
- Contrarian takes on tools, frameworks, and "best practices"
- Founder-to-founder honesty that corporate blogs never publish
- Speed — "we did X in 30 days" outperforms "after years of learning"

### Step 3 — Hard rules for this niche
- NEVER start with "As a founder/PM/engineer..." — overused, skippable
- NEVER use "leveraged" or "utilized" — sounds like a press release
- NEVER write hooks that could apply to any industry — be specific to SaaS
- ALWAYS tie to a measurable outcome when possible (ARR, churn %, conversion rate)
- ALWAYS match language to seniority — founder hooks ≠ developer hooks

### Step 4 — Line break strategy for tech audience
They scan faster than other audiences. Format:
```
Line 1: Metric, outcome, or bold claim (under 8 words)
[blank]
Line 2: The twist or context (1 sentence)
[blank]
Line 3: Open loop ("here's what we found" / "thread below:")
```

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL HOOKS

### Why Emotion Beats Information
LinkedIn's algorithm doesn't reward information delivery — it rewards emotional intensity. The algorithm measures **engagement velocity**: how quickly and intensely people interact after you post. But here's what most people miss: the *type* of engagement matters enormously.

- Comments > Likes
- Saves > Comments  
- Shares > Saves
- Comment depth matters — a back-and-forth thread is worth more than a dozen surface-level replies

This means the algorithm is optimizing for **emotional intensity**. A post that makes someone feel mildly interested gets a like. A post that makes them feel strongly — agreement, disagreement, recognition, aspiration — gets a comment. A post that makes them feel so strongly they want others to see it gets a share.

The algorithm doesn't care what emotion you're triggering. It only cares how strongly you're triggering it.

Your job isn't to inform. It's to make people feel something powerful enough that they can't scroll past.

### The 6 Emotional Triggers

**1. Identity Validation**
People don't engage with content — they engage with reflections of themselves. The most powerful trigger is making someone feel *seen*. When you articulate something they've thought but never said, experienced but never had language for, they feel a rush of recognition that's almost impossible not to respond to.

**2. Status Signaling**
People share content not just because it's good, but because sharing it makes them look good. When you create content, you're not just creating something to consume — you're creating a social object people use to signal something to their network. The question isn't "is this valuable?" It's "what does sharing this say about the person who shares it?"

**3. Tribal Belonging**
Humans are tribal. Content that clearly delineates who it's for and who it's not for triggers deep psychological responses. When you say "this is for founders who..." or contrast "us" vs "them," you create identity categories people want to belong to — and they'll engage to demonstrate they're part of "us."

**4. Productive Discomfort**
Comfort doesn't create action — discomfort does. This is different from negativity. Productive discomfort is discomfort with a path forward. You're creating awareness of a gap between where someone is and where they could be, with an implied path to close it. This triggers cognitive dissonance: they must either reject your premise, accept it and change, or do nothing.

**5. Curiosity Gap**
The psychological tension created when someone knows information exists but doesn't have it. This is why clickbait works (even when we hate it). The key: intensity matters more than novelty. A strong curiosity gap includes specific numbers/outcomes, implied insider knowledge, tension between expectation and reality, and clear value for closing the gap.

**6. Aspiration and Possibility**
Pure emotional fuel — making people believe something they want is actually possible for them. This creates active engagement because it shifts beliefs about what they can achieve. The key is believability: "how I made $10M" triggers skepticism, "how I went from 0 to $42k MRR in 90 days" triggers aspiration. Impressive but not impossible.

### The Information vs Provocation Distinction

This is the single most important distinction in viral content:

```
❌ INFORMATION: "Here are 5 tips for improving your churn rate..."
✅ PROVOCATION: "Your churn isn't a product problem. It's a psychology problem.
   
Your users aren't leaving because your feature set is weak.
They're leaving because you designed your onboarding for people who already understood your product — not for humans who need to get it in 30 seconds.
   
Here's what that looks like..."
```

The first delivers value. The second creates recognition. The first teaches. The second *makes people feel something*.

The content that books 50+ calls monthly isn't more valuable. It's more emotionally triggering.

### The Authenticity Filter

Before posting any emotionally charged hook, run it through this filter:

1. **Is this true?** Not "could this be true" — is this actually true based on your direct experience? Emotional content only works when grounded in reality. The moment someone suspects you're exaggerating, trust evaporates.

2. **Would you say this in person?** Imagine you're at dinner with smart people in your industry. Would you say this exact thing in this exact way? If it would feel weird or try-hard in person, it will feel that way on LinkedIn too.

3. **Are you earning the emotion?** Emotion should be proportional to the point you're making. Extreme language for minor topics feels ridiculous. Stronger language is earned only when the stakes are real.

4. **Does this serve the reader or serve you?** Guru content is self-serving disguised as helpful. Real content is genuinely oriented toward helping the reader, even when emotionally charged.

## BAD HOOK vs GOOD HOOK COMPARISONS

### Hook #6 — Not What's Stopping You
❌ **Bad:**
```
Most SaaS founders know how to grow their business.
The problem is they don't take enough action.
```
*Why it fails: Preachy, vague, and condescending — this audience hates being lectured*

✅ **Good:**
```
Most SaaS founders know exactly what their churn problem is.

The problem isn't knowledge. It's that fixing churn means admitting the product isn't sticky enough. And nobody wants to have that meeting.
```
*Why it works: Emotionally accurate, names the real blocker (ego/fear), creates recognition*

---

### Hook #9 — Costly Mistakes
❌ **Bad:**
```
We made some big mistakes in year one of our SaaS.
Cost us a lot of time and money.
```
*Why it fails: "A lot" means nothing to a SaaS audience — they think in ARR and runway*

✅ **Good:**
```
We burned $180k and 9 months building a feature nobody asked for.

Not because we were stupid. Because we asked "can we build this?" instead of "should we?"
```
*Why it works: Real number, honest self-reflection, lands the lesson in the hook*

```

It's failing because you're measuring the wrong things entirely.

```

---

### 2. "Stop This" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
Never start your [product roadmap / sprint / OKRs] with [common bad practice].

You're not [Stripe/Notion/Figma]. And [reason the practice that works at scale kills early-stage].
```

**Examples:**
```
Never build a feature because a big customer asked for it.

You're not their product team. And one loud enterprise voice will steer your roadmap off a cliff.
```

```
Never ship without a success metric defined first.

You're not doing research. And "we'll know if it worked when we see it" is how good teams waste quarters.
```

---

### 3. "You're Not Fooling Anyone" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
Are you [doing the SaaS equivalent of cargo cult behaviour]?

Just [copying what successful companies did at scale] and hoping [it applies to your stage]?
```

**Examples:**
```
Are you building a second product because the first one is hard to grow?

Just adding features instead of solving the distribution problem?
```

```
Are you hiring a Head of Sales because you haven't figured out founder-led sales yet?

Just delegating the problem instead of solving it?
```

---

### 4. "You Need This" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
SaaS founders: You need [underrated practice].

Not because the playbook says so. Because without it, you're flying blind at [critical stage].
```

**Examples:**
```
B2B SaaS founders: You need a churn interview process before you need a growth hire.

Not because retention is more important than acquisition. Because you can't fix a leaky bucket by pouring more water in.
```

---

### 5. "Rising Trend That's a Problem" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
There's a growing trend in SaaS to [trendy thing], [get shallow result], then [wrong conclusion].
```

**Examples:**
```
There's a growing trend in SaaS to add AI to the product, call it "AI-powered," put it on the homepage, and call it a feature.

Meanwhile the core product still has a 60% drop-off at step 3 of onboarding.
```

```
There's a growing habit among early-stage founders to optimise for Series A metrics before they have product-market fit.

The result: clean dashboards. Confused customers. Confused investors.
```

---

### 6. "Not What's Stopping You" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
Most [SaaS founders / PMs / growth teams] know exactly [common knowledge].

The problem isn't [what they blame].
```

**Examples:**
```
Most SaaS founders know exactly how to reduce churn.

The problem isn't strategy. It's that reducing churn requires honest conversations about why people leave — and those conversations are uncomfortable to have internally.
```

```
Most growth teams know exactly what their activation metric should be.

The problem isn't the metric. It's that nobody owns the path to it across product, marketing, and CS.
```

---

### 7. "Self Sabotage" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
Stop [specific SaaS sabotage], [related action], then [rationalising it as strategy].

It's [specific cost this creates].
```

**Examples:**
```
Stop building the enterprise version of your product before a single SMB customer is happy with what you have.

It's scope creep dressed as ambition.
```

```
Stop A/B testing your pricing page when your conversion problem is actually in the trial experience.

It's optimisation theatre. And it costs you months.
```

---

### 8. "Mistake You're Probably Making" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
If you run [specific type of SaaS / at specific stage], you're probably making this mistake:

[Specific, non-obvious, painful mistake]
```

**Examples:**
```
If you're a B2B SaaS founder pre-$1M ARR, you're probably making this mistake:

Hiring a marketing team before you can explain why your last 5 customers chose you.
```

```
If you're a product manager at a growth-stage SaaS, you're likely making this mistake:

Writing user stories based on what customers said they want instead of what they actually do.
```

---

### 9. "Costly Mistakes" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
We made [X] mistakes in our first [Y] months/years.

([Specific cost in $, ARR, churn, customers, runway])
```

**Examples:**
```
We made 5 product decisions in year 1 that cost us 14 months of runway.

None of them felt like mistakes at the time. That's the problem.
```

```
I ignored churn for 6 months while obsessing over new MRR.

Lost $340k in ARR to preventable churn. The new MRR barely covered it.
```

---

### 10. "Shouldn't Ignore" — SaaS/Tech Version
```
Most [SaaS founders / PMs / investors] don't talk about [uncomfortable SaaS reality].
```

**Examples:**
```
Most SaaS founders don't talk about the customers they fired — and what those customers had in common.
```

```
Most PMs don't talk about the features they shipped that made the product worse. Only the ones that worked.
```

## 🔥 RAGE BAIT HOOKS — SAAS/TECH

### Type 1 — "The Sacred Tool Kill"
```
[Beloved tool or framework] is [overrated / broken / costing you].

[One line that hints at why without giving it away]
```

**Examples:**
```
OKRs are making your team worse, not better.

Not because goal-setting is bad. Because most teams use OKRs to justify what they were already going to do.
```

```
Your NPS score is lying to you.

The people who loved your product the most rarely fill in surveys. You're measuring the dissatisfied middle.
```

```
"Product-market fit" is the most misused phrase in SaaS.

Most founders declare it based on vibes and a handful of happy customers. Then they scale into the void.
```

---

### Type 2 — "Unpopular Opinion (SaaS)"
```
Unpopular opinion: [Widely held SaaS belief that you can defend disagreeing with]
```

**Examples:**
```
Unpopular opinion: Most early-stage SaaS companies should not have a product roadmap.

A roadmap is a commitment to building things before you know what customers actually need.
```

```
Unpopular opinion: Free trials hurt more SaaS companies than they help.

You're training users to use your product when the stakes are zero. That's not the behaviour you want.
```

```
Unpopular opinion: "Move fast and break things" is advice from people who no longer have to clean up the mess.
```

---

