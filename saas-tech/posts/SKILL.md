## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — Pick the post's ONE job
- **Teach** → share a framework, process, or counterintuitive lesson
- **Confess** → founder honesty — what went wrong and what you learned
- **Challenge** → flip a widely held belief in product, growth, or engineering
- **Celebrate** → milestone or win (use sparingly — must have lessons embedded)
- **Warn** → flag a mistake or trap before others fall into it

### Step 2 — SaaS post formatting rules
- Use **metrics whenever possible** — this audience respects numbers
- Keep paragraphs to 1-2 sentences — they read fast
- Use numbered lists for steps/lessons, arrows (→) for outcomes
- Write at the level of a smart operator, not a thought leader
- Avoid jargon unless you're writing for a technical dev audience
- Ending: either a question ("what's your churn interview process?") or a hard truth statement

### Step 3 — The "Founder Credibility" test
Before publishing, ask: "Does this post say something a generic content creator could also write?"
If yes — add one specific detail that only someone who has actually done this would know.

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL POSTS

### Hook to Body: The Emotional Arc
The hook captures attention. The body *sustains* the emotional journey. Most posts lose people in paragraph 3 because they shift from emotion to information too quickly.

The rule: **Don't explain the emotion — amplify it.**

Your hook made them feel something (recognition, curiosity, discomfort). Now your body must:
1. Deepen that feeling with proof, examples, or story
2. Create a slight tension/delay before revealing the solution
3. End with a clear action or question that forces them to engage

### The "Emotion First, Information Second" Formula

Every viral post follows this structure:

```
[Emotional Hook] → "your churn is broken"
        ↓
[Emotional Proof] → "here's what we found analyzing 2,400 companies"
        ↓
[Emotional Twist] → "it's not the product. it's the psychology."
        ↓
[Solution + Emotion] → "here's what actually works (and why)"
        ↓
[Action/CTA] → "what's your churn interview process?"
```

The mistake: jumping to "here's the framework" before they've felt why they need it.

### The Curiosity Gap Throughout
The hook opened a curiosity gap. The body must *widen* it before closing it.

- Hook: "The LinkedIn post that booked me 47 calls"
- Body first: Show the result again, add proof, create tension ("but it wasn't always like this")
- Body second: Walk through the journey, the failures, the realization
- Body third: Give the framework as the *climax*, not the opening

### Social Proof Stack
In SaaS, social proof isn't just "we worked with X clients." It's:
- Specific metrics ("42% reduction in churn")
- Before/after comparisons
- Named results ("one client went from 12% to 4% churn in 90 days")
- Proof that can be verified

### The CTA Psychology
Endings drive action. In SaaS:
- Questions work ("what's your ___?")
- Challenges work ("try this for 30 days and report back")
- Invitations work ("comment ___ and I'll send you ___")

## RECREATED POST TEMPLATES

### Template 1 — "The Dumb Thing Everyone Does" (SaaS version)
```
[Common SaaS practice] is expensive and we don't talk about it enough.

[What teams do]

[The result they get — or think they get]

[The real cost — in ARR, time, team morale, or churn]

I [stopped doing / never did] this specific thing.

[What I do instead — specific, actionable]

[The measurable result]

Stop [the dumb thing].

Your [ARR / retention / team] will thank you.
```

**Example:**
```
Building features to win enterprise deals is expensive and we don't talk about it enough.

A prospect asks for SSO. You build it. They sign. You repeat.

Six months later you have a product shaped by 4 enterprise deals and 200 SMB customers who can't use it anymore.

I haven't added a custom enterprise feature in 18 months.

Every feature request goes into the same queue. Evaluated on the same criteria.

We're retaining 94% of SMB customers. That's the business.

Stop letting your biggest logos run your roadmap.

The customers who shout loudest are rarely the ones who'll be there in year 3.
```

### Template 5 — "Fix Broken Thinking" (SaaS thought leadership)
```
Why do [SaaS founders / PMs / growth teams] struggle with [specific problem]?

Because they believe [widely held misconception].

The truth:

[Why the problem actually exists — 2-3 honest lines]

The fix isn't [what everyone tries].

It's [the real solution — stated simply and confidently].

Here's how to think about it:

→ [Mental model or reframe 1]
→ [Mental model or reframe 2]
→ [Mental model or reframe 3]

When you [plan your roadmap / run your growth loop / hire your team], ask:

→ [Strategic question 1]
→ [Strategic question 2]
→ [Strategic question 3]

Instead of [common broken approach], try:

→ [Better approach 1]
→ [Better approach 2]
→ [Better approach 3]

[Final line that makes the reader feel smarter — not lectured]
```

**Example:**
```
Why do early-stage SaaS founders struggle to retain customers past month 3?

Because they believe onboarding ends when the user logs in for the first time.

The truth:

Onboarding ends when the user gets their first meaningful result from your product. For most SaaS, that moment is never deliberately engineered.

The fix isn't more tooltips or product tours.

It's defining what "first value moment" looks like — and building everything backwards from there.

Here's how to think about it:

→ Your activation metric should describe a behaviour, not a session
→ Time-to-value is a product KPI, not an onboarding metric
→ The user who churns in month 3 made the decision to leave in week 2

When you audit your onboarding, ask:

→ What's the minimum a user needs to do to feel this was worth it?
→ How long does it currently take them to get there?
→ What's the biggest thing standing between signup and that moment?

Instead of adding a product tour, try:

→ Remove one step from your onboarding flow
→ Send one email at hour 24 that asks "did you get X done yet?"
→ Define "activated" as a specific action, not a login

The users who churn fastest never got to feel what your product is actually for.

That's an onboarding problem, not a churn problem.
```

## BAD POST vs GOOD POST COMPARISON

❌ **Bad SaaS post:**
```
Growth is hard.

Here are 5 tips to grow your SaaS:
1. Know your customer
2. Build a great product
3. Focus on retention
4. Hire the right people
5. Be consistent

Hope this helps!
```
*Why it fails: Could have been written by anyone. Zero specificity. No story. No stakes. No credibility.*

✅ **Good SaaS post:**
```
We went from 8% monthly churn to 2.1% in 90 days.

Not by adding features. Not by changing pricing.

By running 12 exit interviews with churned customers in week 1.

Here's what we found:

Every single churn had one of 3 root causes:
→ Never reached their "first win" moment in the product
→ The person who championed us internally left the company
→ We were solving the problem but they couldn't explain our value to their boss

Here's what we changed:

1. Rebuilt onboarding around the "first win" — one specific action, in under 10 minutes
2. Added a stakeholder mapping step to our sales process (who owns budget, who influences?)
3. Built a one-pager for every customer to share internally — in their language, not ours

None of this was clever. All of it came directly from the exit interviews.

The customers who leave are your most honest product feedback.

You just have to be willing to call them.
```
*Why it works: Real numbers, real process, specific lessons, honest, useful*

## DO'S AND DON'TS

✅ **DO:**
- Use real metrics (even small ones — honesty beats inflated claims)
- Write from experience, not theory
- Name the stage you're writing for ($0-1M / $1-5M / post Series A)
- Leave the reader with something they can do TODAY

❌ **DON'T:**
- Use "synergy," "disruptive," "scalable" — red flags for this audience
- Post about what worked for Stripe/Slack/Notion and imply it'll work for a 5-person startup
- Write generic "10 growth tips" without a specific angle
- Forget the ending — SaaS readers want a payoff
