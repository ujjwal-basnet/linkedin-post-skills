# LinkedIn Hook Templates — Agency & Freelancing

## SKILL OVERVIEW
Use this file for hooks targeting **agency owners, freelancers, consultants, and independent professionals**.
This includes: creative agencies, marketing agencies, dev shops, solo consultants, fractional executives, and service-based business owners.

---

## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — Know this audience's lived reality
Agency/freelance readers are dealing with:
- **Feast-or-famine revenue** — they know this feeling viscerally
- **Scope creep** — a constant tax on their time and sanity
- **Underpricing** — almost universally, especially in early years
- **Client dependency** — fear of losing the one big client
- **Positioning ambiguity** — "I do a bit of everything" trap
- **The comparison trap** — watching other agencies post big numbers

### Step 2 — What triggers this audience
- Specific dollar/revenue numbers ("from $3k/month to $22k/month")
- Relatable client horror stories (carefully handled)
- Permission to charge more, say no, fire clients
- Systems and processes that reduce chaos
- The "I wish someone had told me this" framing

### Step 3 — Hard rules for this niche
- NEVER be vague about the niche (freelancer ≠ agency owner ≠ consultant)
- ALWAYS use a revenue number if you have one — it's the universal credibility signal here
- Avoid lecturing — this audience has heard "know your worth" a thousand times
- The most powerful posts here are honest stories, not frameworks
- This audience is tired of hustle-bro energy — they want calm professionalism

### Step 4 — Tone calibration
Write like: **An experienced operator who made the mistakes and figured out the other side.**
Not like: A hype coach telling them they just need to "think bigger."

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL HOOKS

### Why Emotion Beats Information
LinkedIn's algorithm doesn't reward information delivery — it rewards emotional intensity. The algorithm measures **engagement velocity**: how quickly and intensely people interact after you post. But here's what most people miss: the *type* of engagement matters enormously.

- Comments > Likes
- Saves > Comments  
- Shares > Saves
- Comment depth matters — a back-and-forth thread is worth more than a dozen surface-level replies

This means the algorithm is optimizing for **emotional intensity**. A post that makes someone feel mildly interested gets a like. A post that makes them feel strongly — agreement, disagreement, recognition, aspiration — gets a comment. A post that makes them feel so strongly they want others to see it gets a share.

The algorithm doesn't care what emotion you're triggering. It only cares how strongly you're triggering it.

Your job isn't to inform. It's to make people feel something powerful enough that they can't scroll past.

### The 6 Emotional Triggers

**1. Identity Validation**
People don't engage with content — they engage with reflections of themselves. The most powerful trigger is making someone feel *seen*. When you articulate something they've thought but never said, experienced but never had language for, they feel a rush of recognition that's almost impossible not to respond to. This is especially powerful for freelancers who often feel misunderstood by the 9-5 world.

**2. Status Signaling**
People share content not just because it's good, but because sharing it makes them look good. When you create content, you're not just creating something to consume — you're creating a social object people use to signal something to their network. The question isn't "is this valuable?" It's "what does sharing this say about the person who shares it?"

**3. Tribal Belonging**
Humans are tribal. Content that clearly delineates who it's for and who it's not for triggers deep psychological responses. When you say "this is for freelancers who..." or contrast "us" vs "them," you create identity categories people want to belong to — and they'll engage to demonstrate they're part of "us."

**4. Productive Discomfort**
Comfort doesn't create action — discomfort does. This is different from negativity. Productive discomfort is discomfort with a path forward. You're creating awareness of a gap between where someone is and where they could be, with an implied path to close it. This triggers cognitive dissonance: they must either reject your premise, accept it and change, or do nothing.

**5. Curiosity Gap**
The psychological tension created when someone knows information exists but doesn't have it. This is why clickbait works (even when we hate it). The key: intensity matters more than novelty. A strong curiosity gap includes specific numbers/outcomes, implied insider knowledge, tension between expectation and reality, and clear value for closing the gap.

**6. Aspiration and Possibility**
Pure emotional fuel — making people believe something they want is actually possible for them. This creates active engagement because it shifts beliefs about what they can achieve. The key is believability: in freelancing, "$3k to $22k/month" feels more achievable than "$10M." Impressive but not impossible.

### The Information vs Provocation Distinction

This is the single most important distinction in viral content:

```
❌ INFORMATION: "Here are 5 tips for raising your freelance rates..."
✅ PROVOCATION: "You're not stuck because you lack confidence.
   
You're stuck because raising rates requires having a conversation — and most freelancers were never taught how to have that conversation without losing the client.
   
The problem isn't the number on your invoice. It's the fear of the dialogue that comes after it.
   
Here's what that conversation actually sounds like:"
```

The first delivers value. The second creates recognition. The first teaches. The second *makes people feel something*.

The freelancers booking 30+ calls monthly aren't more skilled. They're more emotionally triggering.

### The Authenticity Filter

Before posting any emotionally charged hook, run it through this filter:

1. **Is this true?** Not "could this be true" — is this actually true based on your direct experience? Emotional content only works when grounded in reality. The moment someone suspects you're exaggerating, trust evaporates.

2. **Would you say this in person?** Imagine you're at dinner with smart people in your industry. Would you say this exact thing in this exact way? If it would feel weird or try-hard in person, it will feel that way on LinkedIn too.

3. **Are you earning the emotion?** Emotion should be proportional to the point you're making. Extreme language for minor topics feels ridiculous. Stronger language is earned only when the stakes are real.

4. **Does this serve the reader or serve you?** Guru content is self-serving disguised as helpful. Real content is genuinely oriented toward helping the reader, even when emotionally charged.

## BAD HOOK vs GOOD HOOK COMPARISONS

### Hook #9 — Costly Mistakes
❌ **Bad:**
```
I made some big mistakes when I started freelancing.
Here are some lessons I learned the hard way.
```
*Why it fails: No stakes, no numbers, no reason to keep reading*

✅ **Good:**
```
I lost £67,000 in unbilled work in my first 3 years of freelancing.

Not from bad clients. From bad systems and worse boundaries.
```
*Why it works: Specific number, shifts blame from client to self (more credible), instantly relatable*

---

### Hook #6 — Not What's Stopping You
❌ **Bad:**
```
Most freelancers know they should raise their rates.
The problem is they just don't do it.
```
*Why it fails: "Just do it" is not insight — it's the thing they already know and aren't doing*

✅ **Good:**
```
Most freelancers know their rates are too low.

The problem isn't confidence. It's that raising rates requires having a conversation — and most freelancers were never taught how to have that conversation without losing the client.
```
*Why it works: Names the real specific obstacle (the conversation skill, not the confidence)*

## RECREATED HOOK TEMPLATES

### 1. "Not Your Fault" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
Your [pricing / positioning / pipeline / client relationship] is broken — and it's not your fault.

[The industry / common advice / how everyone starts out] set you up for this.

Here's what nobody tells you until it's already cost you [something painful]:
```

**Examples:**
```
Your rates are too low — and it's not because you lack confidence.

It's because every freelancing resource tells you to "research the market rate" without telling you the market rate is artificially low because everyone else is doing the same thing.
```

```
Your agency is stuck at the same revenue — and it's not because you need better clients.

It's because you've built a business around time, not outcomes. And there's a ceiling on time.
```

---

### 2. "Stop This" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
Never [specific destructive agency/freelance behaviour].

You're not [someone who has the luxury of doing that thing]. And [specific reason it's costing you].
```

**Examples:**
```
Never send a proposal without a discovery call first.

You're not a vending machine. And clients who skip the conversation almost always have the wrong expectations before the project starts.
```

```
Never discount your rate to win a client.

You're not a clearance rack. And the client who needs a discount on day one will need one every invoice after.
```

```
Never say yes to a project before you know what success looks like to the client.

You're not a mind reader. And "I'll know it when I see it" is how great work dies in revision hell.
```

---

### 3. "Not Fooling Anyone" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
Are you [building a business or building a slightly stressful job]?

Just [doing the thing that keeps revenue coming in] and hoping [the thing you actually want] takes care of itself?
```

**Examples:**
```
Are you running an agency or a very stressful solo operation with a few contractors?

Just taking every project that pays and hoping "scale" happens naturally?
```

```
Are you "niching down" on your website while still saying yes to anything that lands in your inbox?

Just hoping the right clients start showing up without actually changing what you do?
```

---

### 4. "You Need This" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
[Freelancers / Agency owners / Consultants]: You need [specific underrated business practice].

Not because [the obvious reason]. Because without it, [specific, painful consequence they haven't named yet].
```

**Examples:**
```
Freelancers: You need a "not right now" response template.

Not because you're too busy. Because every client you say an unstructured "no" to is a referral relationship you're potentially closing.
```

```
Agency owners: You need a monthly financial review that isn't just looking at your bank balance.

Not because accountants say so. Because the difference between 30% margin and 8% margin is almost always invisible until it's too late.
```

---

### 5. "Rising Trend" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
There's a growing trend among [freelancers / agencies / consultants] to [trendy but harmful thing], [get surface-level result], then [wrong conclusion].
```

**Examples:**
```
There's a growing trend among freelancers to productise their services, put a price on a landing page, and call it "passive income."

Then wonder why they're still chasing custom scope every week for the same clients.
```

```
There's a rising habit among agency owners to hire before they have systems, then spend more time managing people than doing client work.

It's "scaling" with a different kind of chaos.
```

---

### 6. "Not What's Stopping You" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
Most [freelancers / agency owners / consultants] know exactly [what they need to do].

The problem isn't [what they blame].
```

**Examples:**
```
Most freelancers know they need to raise their rates.

The problem isn't confidence. It's never having practiced the conversation that follows the new number.
```

```
Most agency owners know they need better clients.

The problem isn't lead generation. It's that "better" is undefined — and you'll keep attracting the same ones until you decide what you won't accept anymore.
```

```
Most consultants know they should niche down.

The problem isn't clarity. It's that narrowing your audience feels like turning down money — and that feeling is terrifying when your pipeline is thin.
```

---

### 7. "Self Sabotage" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
Stop [specific business self-sabotage], [related action], then [justifying it as smart strategy].

It's [what it's actually costing you].
```

**Examples:**
```
Stop taking on one more project "just to get through this month," hiring to fulfil it, then watching your margin disappear in delivery.

It's not growth. It's survival with extra paperwork.
```

```
Stop undercharging for discovery so you can win the project, then absorbing strategy hours into the project cost.

It's not client service. It's subsidising their business with yours.
```

```
Stop doing free "test" work to prove your value to a potential client.

It's not a strategy. It's a precedent. And clients remember what you did for free when they're deciding what to pay for.
```

---

### 8. "Mistake You're Making" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
If you [run a specific type of agency / work in a specific freelance context], you're probably making this mistake:

[Specific, non-obvious, painful mistake]
```

**Examples:**
```
If you're a freelancer with 2-3 anchor clients, you're probably making this mistake:

Treating "not losing them" as a growth strategy. Retention is not a pipeline.
```

```
If you run a creative agency under 10 people, you're probably making this mistake:

Pricing projects on hours instead of outcomes — and wondering why profitable projects feel like a grind.
```

```
If you've been freelancing for more than 2 years and revenue hasn't grown, you're probably making this mistake:

Waiting for a better client instead of building a better offer.
```

---

### 9. "Costly Mistakes" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
I [made X costly mistakes / lost $X / wasted Y months] doing [specific thing].

Not because [reason that would make them unsympathetic]. Because [reason that makes it relatable and teachable].
```

**Examples:**
```
I left £94,000 on the table in my first 4 years as a freelancer.

Not from bad clients. From never having a value-based pricing conversation with a single one of them.
```

```
I spent 18 months building a service nobody asked for.

Not because I didn't do research. Because I asked "would this be useful?" instead of "would you pay for this tomorrow?"
```

---

### 10. "Shouldn't Ignore" — Agency/Freelancing Version
```
Most [freelancers / agency owners] don't talk about [uncomfortable truth about the business].
```

**Examples:**
```
Most agency owners don't talk about the clients they fired — and how much better the business got immediately after.
```

```
Most freelancers don't talk about the year they hit six figures and realised they'd built a job, not a business.
```

```
Most consultants don't talk about the proposal they're most proud of — the one the client rejected for a cheaper option.
```

## 🔥 RAGE BAIT HOOKS — AGENCY/FREELANCING

### Type 1 — "The Pricing Sacred Cow"
```
Hourly billing is the single biggest reason most freelancers will never build wealth.

It aligns your incentive (earn more) with your client's nightmare (pay more hours).

Here's what works instead:
```

```
"Know your worth" is useless advice if you've never been taught how to demonstrate value in a proposal.

Worth is perception. And perception is built with evidence, not confidence.
```

---

### Type 2 — "Client Relationship Callout"
```
Your best client is your biggest risk.

The day they leave, pause, or "reassess their agency spend" — what does your revenue look like?

Most agency owners don't want to think about this. That's exactly why it catches them.
```

```
The client who takes the most of your time is almost never the one paying you the most.

Draw that chart. It'll be uncomfortable. It'll also be clarifying.
```

---

### Type 3 — "Industry Advice Callout"
```
"Charge what you're worth" is bad advice.

Charge what it's worth to them. Those are very different numbers — and the second one is almost always higher.
```

```
"Just get more clients" is not a business strategy.

More bad-fit clients at your current rates is not growth. It's a louder version of the same problem.
```
