## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — What makes agency/freelancing posts work
- **Specificity of numbers** — lost revenue, gained revenue, hours, clients, projects
- **Honest war stories** — the mistake, the cost, the fix, the lesson
- **Permission posts** — giving the reader permission to do the thing they're afraid to do
- **Practical frameworks** — not vague "think bigger" advice, actual process

### Step 2 — Avoid these patterns
- Success porn without context ("I hit 6 figures!" — says nothing useful)
- Client shaming — even anonymously, it reads as unprofessional to sophisticated readers
- "You just need to believe in yourself" — this audience has been sold that enough
- Overlong lists — if you have 15 tips, pick the 4 that actually matter

### Step 3 — Voice
Write like a battle-tested operator who has made the mistake, fixed it, and wants others to skip the painful part.
Calm. Specific. Generous with information. Zero performance.

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL POSTS

### Hook to Body: The Emotional Arc
The hook captures attention. The body *sustains* the emotional journey. Most posts lose people in paragraph 3 because they shift from emotion to information too quickly.

The rule: **Don't explain the emotion — amplify it.**

Your hook made them feel something (recognition, curiosity, discomfort). Now your body must:
1. Deepen that feeling with proof, examples, or story
2. Create a slight tension/delay before revealing the solution
3. End with a clear action or question that forces them to engage

### The "Emotion First, Information Second" Formula

Every viral post follows this structure:

```
[Emotional Hook] → "You're not stuck because you lack confidence"
        ↓
[Emotional Proof] → "Here's what I lost (£67k in unbilled work)"
        ↓
[Emotional Twist] → "The problem isn't the conversation. It's that you've never had it."
        ↓
[Solution + Emotion] → "Here's what that conversation actually sounds like"
        ↓
[Action/CTA] → "What's your biggest pricing fear? Share it below."
```

The mistake: jumping to "here's the framework" before they've felt why they need it.

### The Curiosity Gap Throughout
The hook opened a curiosity gap. The body must *widen* it before closing it.

- Hook: "I lost £67,000 in unbilled work"
- Body first: Show how it happened — specific situations, specific invoices
- Body second: The realization about systems and boundaries
- Body third: What doing it differently looks like

### The Numbers Speak
For agency/freelancing, numbers *are* social proof:
- Specific loss amounts (£67,000, $40k)
- Specific revenue lifts (from $3k to $22k/month)
- Specific time savings (hours, months)
- Specific client impacts (fired, retained, referrer)

### The CTA Psychology
Endings drive action. In agency/freelancing:
- Questions work ("what's your biggest pricing fear?")
- Challenges work ("try this for 30 days and report back")
- Invitations work ("comment 'SYSTEM' and I'll send you my pricing template")

## BAD POST vs GOOD POST COMPARISON

❌ **Bad agency post:**
```
Want to double your freelance income?

Here are 5 tips:
1. Raise your rates
2. Get better clients
3. Build a niche
4. Market yourself
5. Work smarter not harder

Follow for more tips!
```
*Why it fails: Every single point is something they already know. Zero new information. Zero story. Zero reason to trust the author.*

✅ **Good agency post:**
```
I doubled my freelance income by doing one thing.

Not "getting better clients."

Not "building a niche" (though that helped later).

I doubled it by adding one question to every proposal:

"What would it be worth to your business if this worked?"

Then I shut up and listened.

Most clients would answer with a number 5-10x my proposed fee.

After that conversation, my price didn't feel expensive — it felt like a bargain.

I wasn't selling hours anymore. I wasn't selling deliverables.

I was participating in a conversation about value.

The uncomfortable truth: most freelancers never have this conversation.

Not because they can't. Because they're afraid the client will say "it's not worth that much."

They might. But in 4 years of asking this question — that's only happened twice.

What's stopping you from having the value conversation?
```
*Why it works: One specific thing, a concrete script to use, honest vulnerability, engaging question at the end*

```
→ [Concrete outcome]

→ [Concrete outcome]
```

**Example:**
```

→ You can make it easy for existing clients to refer by simply asking them directly

→ A clearer sense of who your business is actually for
→ 3-5 referral conversations started
→ A portfolio of content that pre-qualifies the right people before they ever contact you

Stop saying: "I need to find better clients"

Start saying: "I need to make it easier for the right clients to find me"

The clients are already out there. They just can't tell yet that you're the right fit.
```

### Template 5 — "Fix Broken Thinking" (Agency thought leadership)
```
Why do [freelancers / agency owners / consultants] struggle with [specific problem]?

Because they've been taught that [widely held but flawed belief].

Here's what's actually happening:

[2-3 sentences that name the real dynamic honestly]

The fix isn't [what everyone tries].

It's [real solution — stated directly].

Here's how to think about it:

→ [Reframe 1 — practical, not philosophical]
→ [Reframe 2]
→ [Reframe 3]

When you [face the situation], ask:

→ [Question 1 that creates movement]
→ [Question 2]
→ [Question 3]

Instead of [broken approach], try:

→ [Better approach 1]
→ [Better approach 2]
→ [Better approach 3]

[Closing line — a hard truth stated cleanly]
```

**Example:**
```
Why do freelancers struggle to raise their rates with existing clients?

Because they've been taught it's a confidence problem.

It isn't. It's a communication problem.

Most rate increase conversations fail because the freelancer leads with their cost of living or their experience — both of which are irrelevant to the client.

The client only has one question: "Is it still worth it at the new rate?"

Answer that question before they have to ask it.

Here's how to think about it:

→ The rate conversation is a value conversation — not a negotiation
→ Clients who've seen results have already answered "yes" — they just need to be reminded
→ The worst they can say is no — and that tells you something useful too

When you're preparing a rate increase, ask:

→ What has materially changed in the client's business because of our work?
→ What would it cost them to replace this output another way?
→ What are we doing now that we weren't doing at the original rate?

Instead of: "I need to raise my rates because..."

Try: "Based on [specific result], here's what I'd like to move to — and here's why I think it reflects the value we're creating together."

Confidence doesn't raise rates. Evidence does.
```

## DO'S AND DON'TS

✅ **DO:**
- Use actual numbers — revenue, hours, clients, months, percentages
- Share what actually happened — not the motivational version
- Give one specific thing they can do TODAY
- Write for one type of person (freelancer at 6 months ≠ agency owner at 5 years)

❌ **DON'T:**
- Post "you just need to believe in yourself" energy
- Shame clients even anonymously (it reads as unprofessional)
- List generic advice like "have great processes" without showing what that means
- End with "follow for more" — earn the follow, don't ask for it
