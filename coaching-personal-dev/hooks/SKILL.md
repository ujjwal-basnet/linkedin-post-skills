# LinkedIn Hook Templates — Coaching & Personal Development

## SKILL OVERVIEW
Use this file for hooks targeting audiences in the **coaching and personal development niche**.
This includes: life coaches, business coaches, executive coaches, therapists turned coaches, mindset/productivity creators, and self-improvement content creators.

---

## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — Know this audience's psychology
Coaching/PD readers are:
- **Emotionally literate** — they respond to language that names feelings accurately
- **Self-aware but stuck** — they know they have a problem; they're looking for permission and clarity
- **Skeptical of gurus** — overselling creates instant distrust
- **Identity-driven** — they think in terms of "who I'm becoming," not just "what I'm doing"
- **Motivated by transformation stories** — before/after is powerful here

### Step 2 — What works in this niche
- **Validation hooks** — "finally someone said it" moments
- **Reframes** — renaming what someone is experiencing so they see it differently
- **Pattern interrupts on "hustle culture" advice** — this audience is tired of it
- **Honest vulnerability** — but with a lesson, not just for sympathy
- **Precision emotion** — "the exhaustion that comes from doing everything right and still feeling unfulfilled" beats "burnout"

### Step 3 — What kills hooks in this niche
- Toxic positivity: "Just believe in yourself!"
- Vague spiritual language without practical grounding
- Motivational poster energy — "Your dreams are valid!"
- Oversimplified binary thinking: "Either you're growing or you're dying"
- Starting with "I" (universal LinkedIn rule — especially true here)

### Step 4 — Tone calibration
Coaching/PD posts perform best when they feel like:
> A brilliant, warm friend who happens to know a lot about human behaviour — not a guru, not a therapist, not a hype coach.

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL HOOKS

### Why Emotion Beats Information
LinkedIn's algorithm doesn't reward information delivery — it rewards emotional intensity. The algorithm measures **engagement velocity**: how quickly and intensely people interact after you post. But here's what most people miss: the *type* of engagement matters enormously.

- Comments > Likes
- Saves > Comments  
- Shares > Saves
- Comment depth matters — a back-and-forth thread is worth more than a dozen surface-level replies

This means the algorithm is optimizing for **emotional intensity**. A post that makes someone feel mildly interested gets a like. A post that makes them feel strongly — agreement, disagreement, recognition, aspiration — gets a comment. A post that makes them feel so strongly they want others to see it gets a share.

The algorithm doesn't care what emotion you're triggering. It only cares how strongly you're triggering it.

Your job isn't to inform. It's to make people feel something powerful enough that they can't scroll past.

### The 6 Emotional Triggers

**1. Identity Validation**
People don't engage with content — they engage with reflections of themselves. The most powerful trigger is making someone feel *seen*. When you articulate something they've thought but never said, experienced but never had language for, they feel a rush of recognition that's almost impossible not to respond to. This is especially powerful in coaching/PD where people are seeking language for their inner experiences.

**2. Status Signaling**
People share content not just because it's good, but because sharing it makes them look good. When you create content, you're not just creating something to consume — you're creating a social object people use to signal something to their network. The question isn't "is this valuable?" It's "what does sharing this say about the person who shares it?"

**3. Tribal Belonging**
Humans are tribal. Content that clearly delineates who it's for and who it's not for triggers deep psychological responses. When you say "this is for people who..." or contrast "us" vs "them," you create identity categories people want to belong to — and they'll engage to demonstrate they're part of "us."

**4. Productive Discomfort**
Comfort doesn't create action — discomfort does. This is different from negativity. Productive discomfort is discomfort with a path forward. You're creating awareness of a gap between where someone is and where they could be, with an implied path to close it. This triggers cognitive dissonance: they must either reject your premise, accept it and change, or do nothing.

**5. Curiosity Gap**
The psychological tension created when someone knows information exists but doesn't have it. This is why clickbait works (even when we hate it). The key: intensity matters more than novelty. A strong curiosity gap includes specific numbers/outcomes, implied insider knowledge, tension between expectation and reality, and clear value for closing the gap.

**6. Aspiration and Possibility**
Pure emotional fuel — making people believe something they want is actually possible for them. This creates active engagement because it shifts beliefs about what they can achieve. The key is believability: transformation stories work best when the reader can see themselves in the journey. Impressive but not impossible.

### The Information vs Provocation Distinction

This is the single most important distinction in viral content:

```
❌ INFORMATION: "Here are 5 ways to overcome impostor syndrome..."
✅ PROVOCATION: "You're not suffering from impostor syndrome.
   
You're suffering from the gap between who you were taught to be and who you actually are.
   
The feeling that you're 'faking it' isn't a flaw. It's awareness.
And awareness without action is just another way to stay stuck while feeling sophisticated about it.
   
Here's what actually closes that gap:"
```

The first delivers value. The second creates recognition. The first teaches. The second *makes people feel something*.

The content that transforms lives and builds audiences isn't more valuable. It's more emotionally triggering.

### The Authenticity Filter

Before posting any emotionally charged hook, run it through this filter:

1. **Is this true?** Not "could this be true" — is this actually true based on your direct experience? Emotional content only works when grounded in reality. The moment someone suspects you're exaggerating, trust evaporates.

2. **Would you say this in person?** Imagine you're at dinner with smart people in your industry. Would you say this exact thing in this exact way? If it would feel weird or try-hard in person, it will feel that way on LinkedIn too.

3. **Are you earning the emotion?** Emotion should be proportional to the point you're making. Extreme language for minor topics feels ridiculous. Stronger language is earned only when the stakes are real.

4. **Does this serve the reader or serve you?** Guru content is self-serving disguised as helpful. Real content is genuinely oriented toward helping the reader, even when emotionally charged.

## BAD HOOK vs GOOD HOOK COMPARISONS

### Hook #1 — Not Your Fault
❌ **Bad:**
```
You're struggling because nobody taught you how to handle your emotions.

Follow these steps to feel better.
```
*Why it fails: Condescending, clinical, removes personal agency too completely*

✅ **Good:**
```
You're not lazy. You're exhausted from pretending you're fine.

There's a difference — and the solution is completely different too.
```
*Why it works: Precise naming of the feeling, immediate relief, creates curiosity*

---

### Hook #7 — Self Sabotage
❌ **Bad:**
```
Stop self-sabotaging your success.

Here's what you need to change.
```
*Why it fails: Vague, obvious, preachy — the reader already knows they're self-sabotaging*

✅ **Good:**
```
Stop calling it "self-sabotage."

You're not broken. You're protecting yourself from something that once hurt you.

That's worth understanding before you try to "fix" it.
```
*Why it works: Reframes the diagnosis, removes shame, creates deep curiosity*

## RECREATED HOOK TEMPLATES

### 1. "Not Your Fault" — Coaching/PD Version
**Best for:** Opening a teaching post with instant rapport
```
You're not [negative self-label they're applying to themselves].

[The real, external or psychological reason they're struggling].

Here's what's actually going on:
```

**Examples:**
```
You're not unmotivated. You're unclear.

Motivation isn't the problem. Ambiguity is. And nobody teaches you how to get clear.
```

```
You're not bad at relationships. You're applying patterns that made sense in a different chapter of your life.
```

```
You're not behind. You're comparing your chapter 3 to someone else's chapter 12.

Here's why that's costing you more than time:
```

---

### 2. "Stop This" — Coaching/PD Version
```
Stop [specific self-defeating behaviour], [related action], then [common follow-up that makes it worse].

It's not discipline you need. It's [what they actually need].
```

**Examples:**
```
Stop setting goals for the person you want to be

and starting the plan from where you actually are.

Those two people need completely different strategies.
```

```
Stop asking "what should I do with my life?" and expecting clarity to arrive before you start moving.

Clarity comes from action. Not from more reflection.
```

```
Stop researching the same problem you researched last year.

You already know enough. The bottleneck isn't information.
```

---

### 3. "Not Fooling Anyone" — Coaching/PD Version
*Must be warm, not harsh — this audience will shut down if they feel attacked*
```
Are you [specific form of self-deception common in this niche]?

Telling yourself [rationalisation] while [the real pattern continues]?
```

**Examples:**
```
Are you calling it "rest" when it's actually avoidance?

There's a difference. And your body knows which one it is.
```

```
Are you saying you're "not ready yet" — for the same thing you said you weren't ready for a year ago?
```

```
Are you being "kind to yourself" or are you letting yourself off the hook?

Compassion and avoidance feel identical from the inside. Here's how to tell them apart:
```

---

### 4. "You Need This" — Coaching/PD Version
```
[Specific audience]: You need [underrated psychological skill or practice].

Not because it sounds good. Because without it, [specific consequence they recognise].
```

**Examples:**
```
High achievers: You need to learn how to disappoint people on purpose.

Not because it feels good. Because saying yes to everything is making you excellent at other people's lives and exhausted in your own.
```

```
Empaths: You need a boundary that doesn't require an explanation.

Not because people don't deserve one. Because you're spending all your energy justifying your needs instead of just having them.
```

---

### 5. "Rising Trend That's a Problem" — Coaching/PD Version
```
There's a growing trend in [self-help / coaching / wellness] to [popular but problematic approach], [get shallow result], then [wrong conclusion or action].
```

**Examples:**
```
There's a growing trend in personal development to monetise every skill you have, build an audience around every interest, and call burnout "the price of passion."

It isn't. It's just burnout.
```

```
There's a rising habit in the coaching world to package a 3-month trauma into a 90-minute workshop and sell the "healing."

Some things take longer than a weekend retreat. And that's not a failure.
```

---

### 6. "Not What's Stopping You" — Coaching/PD Version
*The single most powerful hook format for this niche*
```
Most [people / high achievers / parents / leaders] know exactly [what they're supposed to do].

The problem isn't [what they blame].
```

**Examples:**
```
Most people know exactly what they need to change.

The problem isn't information. It's that changing means letting go of an identity they've been building for 20 years.
```

```
Most high achievers know they need to slow down.

The problem isn't willpower. It's that slowing down feels indistinguishable from falling behind — and that fear is louder than any logic.
```

```
Most people know they should set better boundaries.

The problem isn't that they don't know how. It's that they've been taught that needing less makes you easier to love.
```

---

### 7. "Self Sabotage" — Coaching/PD Version
*This is the core of the coaching niche — use it with depth, not judgment*
```
Stop [specific self-defeating pattern].

It's not [what they think it is].

It's [the deeper, more compassionate reframe].
```

**Examples:**
```
Stop calling it procrastination.

It's not laziness. It's your nervous system protecting you from the judgment that might come if you try and fail.

That changes everything about how to move forward.
```

```
Stop saying you're "too busy" for the things that matter most to you.

Busy is a choice. It's also the most socially acceptable way to avoid the vulnerability of going after what you actually want.
```

---

### 8. "Mistake You're Making" — Coaching/PD Version
*Frame with compassion — this audience self-flagellates enough already*
```
If you're a [specific person in a specific season], you're probably doing this:

[Specific, compassionately named pattern]

And it makes total sense that you are. Here's what's actually happening:
```

**Examples:**
```
If you're a high achiever going through a hard season, you're probably doing this:

Working harder to outrun the feeling instead of sitting with what the feeling is telling you.
```

```
If you've recently made a big life change, you're probably doing this:

Measuring the new life against the old one. But you haven't given the new one enough time to show you what it's capable of.
```

---

### 9. "Costly Mistakes" — Coaching/PD Version
*The "cost" here is time and emotional energy, not money*
```
I spent [X years/months] [specific behaviour or belief].

It cost me [specific emotional, relational, or life cost — not money].
```

**Examples:**
```
I spent 4 years being the most "productive" I've ever been.

It cost me two close friendships, my sense of humour, and the ability to sit still without feeling guilty.
```

```
I spent 3 years helping everyone around me build their dream life.

Turns out that's a very convenient way to avoid building your own.
```

---

### 10. "Shouldn't Ignore" — Coaching/PD Version
```
Most [coaches / people in this season / high achievers] don't talk about [uncomfortable psychological truth].
```

**Examples:**
```
Most coaches don't talk about the clients they couldn't help — and what that taught them about the limits of coaching.
```

```
Most people in recovery (from anything) don't talk about how much they miss the version of themselves that was struggling.

Identity grief is real. And ignoring it makes it worse.
```

```
Most high achievers don't talk about the moment they got everything they worked for — and felt nothing.
```

## 🔥 RAGE BAIT HOOKS — COACHING/PD NICHE

### Type 1 — "Hustle Culture Callout"
```
"Rise and grind" is advice from people who confused suffering with success.
```

```
The productivity system you've been optimising isn't making you more effective.

It's making you better at avoiding the one thing that would actually change things.
```

```
Discipline is overrated.

Most people who lack discipline don't lack willpower. They lack a compelling reason.

Fix the reason first.
```

---

### Type 2 — "Therapy/Coaching Industry Callout"
```
Not everything is a trauma response.

Some things are just choices. And the difference matters — a lot — for what comes next.
```

```
The self-help industry sells you frameworks for problems that require relationships, not books.

You cannot think your way out of something you haven't felt your way through.
```

---

### Type 3 — "Conventional Wisdom Flip"
```
"Believe in yourself" is terrible advice.

You can't believe your way to competence. You act your way there — and belief follows.
```

```
Journaling won't change your life.

Taking the insight from your journal and acting on it — even once — will.

Most people use journals as emotional landfills. The point is the clarity, not the writing.
```

```
Your comfort zone isn't the problem.

The story you tell about what's outside it is.
```
