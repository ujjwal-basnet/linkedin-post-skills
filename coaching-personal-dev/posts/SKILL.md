## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — The post's emotional arc matters more here than in other niches
Coaching/PD posts should take the reader on a mini journey:
1. **Recognition** — "That's me"
2. **Reframe** — "Oh, I've been looking at this wrong"
3. **Relief or hope** — "There's a way through this"
4. **Action or invitation** — "Here's what to do / what do you think?"

### Step 2 — Voice guidelines
- Write like a wise, warm human — not a life coach brand
- Use "you" and "I" — both build intimacy
- Avoid: "As a coach, I see this all the time..." — sounds clinical
- Prefer: "Something I see again and again..." — more human
- Short sentences for emotional moments. Longer for explanation.

### Step 3 — What makes coaching posts go viral
- **Precision emotion** — the more specifically you name a feeling, the more people feel seen
- **Permission** — giving people permission to feel what they're already feeling
- **Non-obvious reframes** — renaming the problem so it becomes solvable
- **Honest vulnerability** — not performed vulnerability, but earned openness

### Step 4 — The "too safe" trap
Most coaching content is too gentle to create movement. Push the reframe further than feels comfortable. The post that makes you think "is this too much?" is usually the one that resonates most.

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL POSTS

### Hook to Body: The Emotional Arc
The hook captures attention. The body *sustains* the emotional journey. Most posts lose people in paragraph 3 because they shift from emotion to information too quickly.

The rule: **Don't explain the emotion — amplify it.**

Your hook made them feel something (recognition, curiosity, discomfort). Now your body must:
1. Deepen that feeling with proof, examples, or story
2. Create a slight tension/delay before revealing the solution
3. End with a clear action or question that forces them to engage

### The Transformation Arc (Coaching-Specific)
For coaching/PD, the body should follow a transformation structure:

1. **Name the feeling precisely** — Go beyond "burnout" to "the exhaustion that comes from doing everything right and still feeling unfulfilled"
2. **Validate** — "That's not weakness. That's awareness."
3. **Reframe** — "What you're calling failure might be redirection."
4. **Invite** — "Here's what I'd instead..."

### The Curiosity Gap Throughout
The hook opened a curiosity gap. The body must *widen* it before closing it.

- Hook: "You're not lazy. You're exhausted from pretending you're fine."
- Body first: Expand on the exhaustion — specific situations, specific moments
- Body second: The realization that changes everything
- Body third: What doing it differently looks like

### Precision Emotion > Vague Inspiration
In coaching, specificity is empathy. Compare:

```
❌ "You deserve rest."
✅ "The kind of rest where you don't wake up at 3am wondering if you've done enough."
```

The more precisely you name what they feel, the more they feel seen — and the more they engage.

### The CTA Psychology
Endings drive action. In coaching:
- Questions work ("have you felt this?")
- Invitations work ("comment 'yes' if this hit different")
- Challenges work ("try this for 7 days and notice what shifts")

## BAD POST vs GOOD POST COMPARISON

❌ **Bad coaching post:**
```
Feeling stuck?

Here are 5 ways to get unstuck:
1. Take a walk
2. Journal your thoughts
3. Talk to someone you trust
4. Set small goals
5. Be kind to yourself

You've got this! 💪
```
*Why it fails: Generic, patronising, no real insight, reads like a poster at a doctor's office*

✅ **Good coaching post:**
```
Feeling stuck is often not about direction.

It's about identity.

You know where you want to go. But the person who gets there is someone you haven't fully become yet.

And becoming that person requires letting go of some version of who you are now.

That's the part nobody tells you.

Moving forward isn't just a logistics problem. It's a grief problem.

You're grieving the self who doesn't need to change. The self who was fine as they were. The self who had a good excuse.

So before you make another plan or set another goal—

Ask yourself: who do I need to say goodbye to, to move forward?

That's usually where the real work is.

What's one version of yourself you've been holding onto longer than you should?
```
*Why it works: Names the real problem (identity/grief), non-obvious reframe, ends with powerful reflection question*

## RECREATED POST TEMPLATES

### Template 1 — "The Dumb Common Wisdom" (Challenge bad advice)
```
[Piece of common self-help advice] isn't just unhelpful.

It's actively making [the problem] worse.

Here's what actually happens when people follow it:

[Honest, specific description of what goes wrong]

[Why the advice sounds right but isn't]

What I've seen work instead:

[Alternative principle — grounded, not fluffy]

[Specific behaviour or practice]

[Why this works — the actual mechanism]

[Closing reflection or question]
```

**Example:**
```
"Just get out of your comfort zone" isn't just unhelpful.

For most people, it's making anxiety worse.

Here's what actually happens when people follow it:

They white-knuckle through something scary, feel terrible, call it growth, and then need twice as long to recover before they can try again.

The advice sounds right because discomfort IS part of growth. But exposure without safety creates trauma, not development.

What I've found works instead:

Expand your capacity for discomfort before expanding the discomfort itself.

That means: build psychological safety first. With a therapist, a coach, a trusted person, or a very honest journal.

When your nervous system knows you can handle what happens if it goes wrong — "getting uncomfortable" stops feeling like danger and starts feeling like adventure.

Growth isn't the absence of fear. It's doing something while fear is there — but not letting fear be the only voice in the room.

What's the difference between brave and reckless to you?
```

```
You're not stuck. You're not ready. And when you are — you'll move.
```

## DO'S AND DON'TS

✅ **DO:**
- Name emotions specifically — not "sad" but "the specific grief of outgrowing a version of yourself"
- Give permission before giving advice
- End with a question that invites reflection, not just agreement
- Write from your own experience wherever possible
- Let posts breathe — short paragraphs, white space, moments of silence

❌ **DON'T:**
- Use toxic positivity ("you've got this!", "believe in yourself!")
- Write posts that could be on a motivational poster
- Give 10 tips when 3 real ones are more valuable
- Perform vulnerability — share what you've actually learned, not what makes you seem relatable
- End with "hit like if you agree" — it's lazy and it doesn't fit this niche
