## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — Know the post's ONE job
Every post must do exactly one of these things:
- **Teach** → reader learns something actionable
- **Challenge** → reader's belief gets flipped
- **Story** → reader feels something and remembers you
- **List** → reader saves it for later
- **Rant** → reader shares it because it validates their frustration

Pick ONE. Posts that try to do multiple things at once do none of them well.

### Step 2 — Structure rules
- **Line length:** Max 1-2 sentences per paragraph. LinkedIn is not a blog.
- **White space:** Every 2-3 lines, add a blank line. Always.
- **Length:** 150-300 words is the sweet spot. Under 100 = too thin. Over 400 = essay mode (only use for big storytelling posts).
- **Ending:** Always end with a question, a call to comment, or a single punchy statement. Never just stop.

### Step 3 — The body must pay off the hook
If the hook promises "why your emails tank" — the body must answer that. Specifically. Not vaguely. Not with 7 generic tips. With the actual answer.

### Step 4 — Formatting signals
- Use numbers for steps (1. 2. 3.) — they feel like progress
- Use arrows (→) for lists of outcomes or components
- Use dashes (—) for asides or emphasis
- NEVER use bullet points as filler — every bullet must earn its place
- Bold **one key phrase per section** maximum — overbolding kills it

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL POSTS

### Hook to Body: The Emotional Arc
The hook captures attention. The body *sustains* the emotional journey. Most posts lose people in paragraph 3 because they shift from emotion to information too quickly.

The rule: **Don't explain the emotion — amplify it.**

Your hook made them feel something (recognition, curiosity, discomfort). Now your body must:
1. Deepen that feeling with proof, examples, or story
2. Create a slight tension/delay before revealing the solution
3. End with a clear action or question that forces them to engage

### The "Emotion First, Information Second" Formula

Every viral post follows this structure:

```
[Emotional Hook] → "your headlines aren't getting clicks"
        ↓
[Emotional Proof] → "we A/B tested 200+ headlines and here's what we found"
        ↓
[Emotional Twist] → "it's not about being clever. it's about being specific."
        ↓
[Solution + Emotion] → "here's the framework (and why templates kill you)"
        ↓
[Action/CTA] → "what's your hardest headline right now?"
```

The mistake: jumping to "here's the framework" before they've felt why they need it.

### The Curiosity Gap Throughout
The hook opened a curiosity gap. The body must *widen* it before closing it.

- Hook: "Your lead magnet is costing you 90% of conversions"
- Body first: Show the pain again, add specific examples of bad lead magnets
- Body second: Reveal what people think they should do vs what actually works
- Body third: Give the solution as the climax, not the opening

### The Transformation Frame
For marketing/copywriting, the most powerful body structure is **transformation**:

1. **Before** — What they were doing that's costing them (specific examples)
2. **Realization** — The moment everything changed (your aha)
3. **After** — The specific results (conversions, leads, revenue)
4. **How** — The framework they can replicate
5. **CTA** — "Comment ___ and I'll send you ___"

### The CTA Psychology
Endings drive action. In marketing:
- Questions work ("what's your conversion rate?")
- Invitations work ("comment 'TEMPLATES' and I'll send you the swipe file")
- Challenges work ("try this on your next campaign and report back in 7 days")

## BAD POST vs GOOD POST COMPARISONS

### "Don't do this, do this instead"
❌ **Bad:**
```
This week I noticed something interesting about marketing.

Here are 3 ways I wouldn't recommend doing content marketing:
1. Posting without a strategy
2. Not engaging with comments
3. Ignoring analytics

Instead do this:
1. Have a strategy
2. Engage with comments
3. Check your analytics

Remember: consistency is key.
```
*Why it fails: Generic advice anyone could write. Zero specificity. No story. Forgettable.*

✅ **Good:**
```
This week a client showed me their "content strategy."

It was a Notion page with 47 content ideas and zero published posts.

Here's what I told them NOT to do:

1. Don't plan more than 2 weeks ahead
Content ideas expire. Relevance doesn't wait.

2. Don't write for "the algorithm"
The algorithm rewards what real humans share. Write for humans first.

3. Don't measure followers
Measure conversations started. That's the only number that predicts revenue.

This is how I'd do it instead:

1. Publish one post today. Imperfect. Just published.
2. Reply to every comment for 30 minutes after.
3. Write the next post based on what people asked.

The best content strategy is the one you'll actually execute.

What's the biggest thing stopping you from posting consistently?
```
*Why it works: Real story, specific advice, ends with engagement question*

## RECREATED POST TEMPLATES

### Template 1 — "The Dumb Thing Everyone Does"
**Best for:** Positioning against common bad practice. High share potential.

```
[Common practice everyone does] is dumb.

And I mean genuinely, provably, expensively dumb.

[Describe what people do]

[Explain the mediocre result it produces]

[Add the ironic or painful twist]

I [don't do / stopped doing this specific thing].

Never even thought about [doing the dumb thing].

[What I do instead — specific]

[The result I get instead — specific, ideally with a number]

Skip [the dumb thing].

Your [metric/outcome] will thank you.
```

**Marketing/Copywriting example:**
```
Writing new copy before testing your current copy is dumb.

And I mean genuinely, provably, expensively dumb.

Most marketers treat copy like décor — refresh it when they're bored, not when the data says to.

So they write new headlines. New CTAs. New hero sections.

And they have absolutely no idea if the old ones were broken or brilliant.

I haven't written a single new homepage in 6 months.

I've been testing the one we have.

We found 3 words in the CTA that were costing us 22% of our conversions.

Three words.

Stop writing new copy.

Start reading what your current copy is telling you.
```

### Template 2 — "It's Impossible to X Today" (Reframe)
**Best for:** Challenging a popular goal with a smarter alternative. High saves + shares.

```
It's impossible to [achieve popular goal] in [platform/space] today.

Instead, here's what IS achievable:

→ You can [specific action] to [specific outcome]
→ You can [specific action] to [specific outcome]
→ You can [specific action] to [specific outcome]
→ You can [specific action] to [specific outcome]

It'll only take you [realistic time investment].

But in [short timeframe], you'll have:

→ [Desirable outcome]
→ [Desirable outcome]
→ [Desirable outcome]

Stop saying: "I want to [impossible goal]"

Start saying: "I want to [daily specific action] today"

Choose [new strategy] over [old strategy].

P.S. [Engaging personal sign-off or question]
```

**Marketing/Copywriting example:**
```
It's impossible to "go viral" on LinkedIn today.

Instead, here's what IS achievable:

→ You can write one post that gets saved by 50 people in your exact niche
→ You can start 3 real conversations that turn into sales calls
→ You can build a reputation as the person who actually explains [topic] clearly
→ You can get 2 inbound DMs this week from people who want to work with you

It'll only take you 45 minutes a day.

But in 90 days, you'll have:

→ A searchable library of expertise people reference
→ A warm audience who already trusts you before you pitch
→ A pipeline that doesn't depend on cold outreach

Stop saying: "I want to grow my LinkedIn following"

Start saying: "I want to start one real conversation today"

Choose depth over reach.

P.S. What would one real client conversation from LinkedIn be worth to you this month?
```

```
2. [Actionable step]
3. [Actionable step]
```

**Marketing/Copywriting example:**

### Template 6 — "Fix Broken Thinking" (Thought Leadership)
**Best for:** Long-form authority building. Highest save rate of all formats.

```
Why do [specific audience] struggle with [specific problem]?

Because they believe [common misconception].

Here's the truth:

[Summarise why the problem actually exists — 2-3 lines]

The fix isn't [what they think it is].

It's [specific solution stated simply].

Here's how to think about it differently:

→ [Mental reframe]
→ [Mental reframe]
→ [Mental reframe]

When you [plan/write/build/pitch], ask yourself:

→ [Strategic question 1]
→ [Strategic question 2]
→ [Strategic question 3]
→ [Strategic question 4]

Instead of [common approach], try this:

→ [Better approach 1]
→ [Better approach 2]
→ [Better approach 3]

[One closing line that makes the reader feel smarter for having read this]
```

**Marketing/Copywriting example:**
```
Why do marketers struggle to write copy that converts?

Because they believe the copy is the problem.

Here's the truth:

90% of conversion problems are positioning problems. The words are fine. The argument is broken.

The fix isn't better adjectives. It's a clearer reason to act now.

Here's how to think about it:

→ Your copy is a salesperson. Is it making the right argument?
→ Your headline is a handshake. Does it earn the next sentence?
→ Your CTA is a door. Are you asking them to walk through before they're ready?

When you sit down to rewrite copy, ask:

→ What does my buyer fear more than the price?
→ What do they want to tell their boss / partner / themselves about this decision?
→ What objection is killing this on page 2 that I haven't addressed on page 1?
→ What would make this feel like a no-brainer?

Instead of rewriting the headline first, try this:

→ Rewrite the offer first
→ Then rewrite the proof (testimonials, data, specifics)
→ Then rewrite the CTA
→ The headline almost writes itself after that

Copy that doesn't convert isn't bad writing.

It's the last symptom of a broken argument upstream.
```

### Template 9 — "Loss Aversion" Post
**Best for:** High-urgency topics. Performs extremely well when stakes are real.

```
[Threat or negative consequence 1]

[Related threat 2]

[Related threat 3]

But here's the thing most people miss:

[Eye-opening way to avoid these — reframe or specific action]

[Punchy closing line — the one thing to walk away with]
```

**Marketing/Copywriting example:**
```
You're losing sales to a competitor with worse copy.

You're losing them to a brand with half your budget.

You're losing them to someone who simply made it easier to say yes.

But here's the thing most people miss:

The gap isn't talent. It's friction. Every extra word, every vague CTA, every unanswered objection — is a door closing.

The best marketing doesn't shout louder.

It removes every reason not to act.
```

## DO'S AND DON'TS

✅ **DO:**
- Write in the active voice — always
- End every post with a question or a punchy single line (not both)
- Use real numbers, real client examples, real observations
- Format for mobile — assume they're reading on their phone
- Write the ending before the middle — know where you're landing

❌ **DON'T:**
- Use "In today's fast-paced world..." — instant unread
- List 10+ tips — pick the 3 best ones
- End with "What do you think?" — it's lazy. Ask a specific question.
- Explain your joke — if you have to explain it, cut it
- Use passive voice: "mistakes were made" → "I made 3 mistakes"
