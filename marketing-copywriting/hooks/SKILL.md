# LinkedIn Hook Templates — Marketing & Copywriting

## SKILL OVERVIEW
Use this file when writing the **opening hook** for LinkedIn posts in the **marketing or copywriting niche**.
This includes: copywriters, marketing consultants, brand strategists, content marketers, growth marketers, and agency owners.

---

## AGENT INSTRUCTIONS (DETAILED)

### Step 1 — Know your Marketing/Copywriting audience's psychology
This audience is:
- **Skeptical of fluff** — they've seen too many "magic formulas" and "secret hacks"
- **Results-driven** — they care about conversions, ROI, and measurable impact
- **Status-conscious** — they want to sound smart and ahead of trends
- **Secretly insecure** — imposter syndrome runs high in this niche
- **Copy-entry** — they're drawn to clever wordplay and aha moments

### Step 2 — What triggers Marketing/Copywriting readers specifically
- Buzzwords they've heard ad nauseum (ROI, viral, growth hacking, disruption)
- Situations they've lived (client revisions, scope creep, "just make it pop")
- Debunked frameworks that keep resurfacing
- Industry pet peeves (stock photos, corporate speak, "brand voice")
- Speed — "I wrote this in 30 minutes" outperforms "after years of practice"

### Step 3 — Hard rules for this niche
- NEVER start with "As a copywriter/marketer..." — overused, skippable
- NEVER use "synergy" or "leverage" — sounds like corporate sludge
- NEVER write hooks that could apply to any industry — be specific to marketing
- ALWAYS tie to a measurable outcome when possible (conversions, click-through, opens)
- ALWAYS match language to the platform — email hooks ≠ LinkedIn hooks

### Step 4 — Line break strategy for marketing audience
They scan fast but appreciate clever turns of phrase. Format:
```
Line 1: Bold claim or counter-intuitive statement (under 10 words)
[blank]
Line 2: The setup or proof (1 sentence)
[blank]
Line 3: Open loop ("here's the blueprint:" / "let me explain:")
```

## THE PSYCHOLOGY OF VIRAL HOOKS

### Why Emotion Beats Information
LinkedIn's algorithm doesn't reward information delivery — it rewards emotional intensity. The algorithm measures **engagement velocity**: how quickly and intensely people interact after you post. But here's what most people miss: the *type* of engagement matters enormously.

- Comments > Likes
- Saves > Comments  
- Shares > Saves
- Comment depth matters — a back-and-forth thread is worth more than a dozen surface-level replies

This means the algorithm is optimizing for **emotional intensity**. A post that makes someone feel mildly interested gets a like. A post that makes them feel strongly — agreement, disagreement, recognition, aspiration — gets a comment. A post that makes them feel so strongly they want others to see it gets a share.

The algorithm doesn't care what emotion you're triggering. It only cares how strongly you're triggering it.

Your job isn't to inform. It's to make people feel something powerful enough that they can't scroll past.

### The 6 Emotional Triggers

**1. Identity Validation**
People don't engage with content — they engage with reflections of themselves. The most powerful trigger is making someone feel *seen*. When you articulate something they've thought but never said, experienced but never had language for, they feel a rush of recognition that's almost impossible not to respond to.

**2. Status Signaling**
People share content not just because it's good, but because sharing it makes them look good. When you create content, you're not just creating something to consume — you're creating a social object people use to signal something to their network. The question isn't "is this valuable?" It's "what does sharing this say about the person who shares it?"

**3. Tribal Belonging**
Humans are tribal. Content that clearly delineates who it's for and who it's not for triggers deep psychological responses. When you say "this is for marketers who..." or contrast "us" vs "them," you create identity categories people want to belong to — and they'll engage to demonstrate they're part of "us."

**4. Productive Discomfort**
Comfort doesn't create action — discomfort does. This is different from negativity. Productive discomfort is discomfort with a path forward. You're creating awareness of a gap between where someone is and where they could be, with an implied path to close it. This triggers cognitive dissonance: they must either reject your premise, accept it and change, or do nothing.

**5. Curiosity Gap**
The psychological tension created when someone knows information exists but doesn't have it. This is why clickbait works (even when we hate it). The key: intensity matters more than novelty. A strong curiosity gap includes specific numbers/outcomes, implied insider knowledge, tension between expectation and reality, and clear value for closing the gap.

**6. Aspiration and Possibility**
Pure emotional fuel — making people believe something they want is actually possible for them. This creates active engagement because it shifts beliefs about what they can achieve. The key is believability: "how I made $10M" triggers skepticism, "how I went from 0 to $42k MRR in 90 days" triggers aspiration. Impressive but not impossible.

### The Information vs Provocation Distinction

This is the single most important distinction in viral content:

```
❌ INFORMATION: "Here are 5 tips for writing better headlines..."
✅ PROVOCATION: "Your headlines aren't getting clicked because they sound like every other headline.
   
You followed the templates. You personalized the first line. You kept it short.
And you got ignored. Just like everyone else who followed the same templates.
   
Here's the uncomfortable truth: when everyone follows the 'best practices,' best practices become average practices.
   
The copywriters booking 30+ leads monthly from LinkedIn aren't following templates. They're writing headlines that sound like they came from a human who actually researched and actually cares."
```

The first delivers value. The second creates recognition. The first teaches. The second *makes people feel something*.

The content that books 50+ calls monthly isn't more valuable. It's more emotionally triggering.

### The Authenticity Filter

Before posting any emotionally charged hook, run it through this filter:

1. **Is this true?** Not "could this be true" — is this actually true based on your direct experience? Emotional content only works when grounded in reality. The moment someone suspects you're exaggerating, trust evaporates.

2. **Would you say this in person?** Imagine you're at dinner with smart people in your industry. Would you say this exact thing in this exact way? If it would feel weird or try-hard in person, it will feel that way on LinkedIn too.

3. **Are you earning the emotion?** Emotion should be proportional to the point you're making. Extreme language for minor topics feels ridiculous. Stronger language is earned only when the stakes are real.

4. **Does this serve the reader or serve you?** Guru content is self-serving disguised as helpful. Real content is genuinely oriented toward helping the reader, even when emotionally charged.

## BAD HOOK vs GOOD HOOK COMPARISONS

### Hook #6 — Not What's Stopping You
❌ **Bad:**
```
Most marketers know how to write better copy.
The problem is they don't take enough action.
```
*Why it fails: Preachy, vague, and condescending — this audience hates being lectured*

✅ **Good:**
```
Most copywriters know exactly why their client's copy isn't converting.

The problem isn't the headline. It's that they're afraid to be bold enough to offend someone — and bold is what cuts through.
```
*Why it works: Emotionally accurate, names the real blocker (fear), creates recognition*

---

### Hook #9 — Costly Mistakes
❌ **Bad:**
```
We made some big mistakes in our first year as an agency.
Cost us a lot of money.
```
*Why it fails: "A lot" means nothing to a marketing audience — they think in hours and revenue*

✅ **Good:**
```
We wrote 47 pieces of "thought leadership" content that generated exactly 2 leads.

Not because the writing was bad. Because we confused being interesting with being useful.
```
*Why it works: Real number, honest self-reflection, lands the lesson in the hook*

### 2. "Stop This" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
Never start a [campaign / piece of copy / client presentation] with [common bad practice].

You're not [famous brand]. And [reason the practice that works at scale kills early-stage efforts]:
```

**Examples:**
```
Never write a headline before you've written the body copy.

You're not writing a billboard. And death by a thousand disclaimers is how good offers die.
```

```
Never pitch "content marketing" to a client who needs sales by Friday.

You're not doing branding. And "build trust first" is how agencies starve while waiting for the check.
```

---

### 3. "You're Not Fooling Anyone" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
Are you [doing the marketing equivalent of cargo cult behaviour]?

Just [copying what big brands did] and hoping [it works for your budget]?
```

**Examples:**
```
Are you using "innovation" and "disruption" in your brand messaging?

Just copying startup TED Talks and hoping big words make you sound credible?
```

```
Are you A/B testing your way to mediocrity?

Just testing variations of the same mediocre offer and wondering why neither wins?
```

---

### 4. "You Need This" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
Marketers: You need [underrated practice].

Not because the playbook says so. Because without it, you're wasting budgets at [critical stage]:
```

**Examples:**
```
Copywriters: You need to study direct response masters before you study "brand" storytellers.

Not because brand is useless. Because if you can't sell, you're just entertainment.
```

```
Marketers: You need a swipe file before you need another course.

Not because learning is bad. Because copying what works beats hoping inspiration strikes.
```

---

### 5. "Rising Trend That's a Problem" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
There's a growing trend in marketing to [trendy thing], [get shallow result], then [wrong conclusion]:
```

**Examples:**
```
There's a growing trend in marketing to replace copywriters with AI, call it "efficiency," and call it a win.

Meanwhile the output reads like a robot trying to sound human. Which, y'know, it is.
```

```
There's a growing habit among agencies to optimise for "engagement" before they have a conversion path.

The result: viral tweets. Zero pipeline. Confused clients asking why the views didn't become sales.
```

---

### 6. "Not What's Stopping You" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
Most [marketers / copywriters / agencies] know exactly [common knowledge].

The problem isn't [what they blame]:
```

**Examples:**
```
Most copywriters know exactly why their client's landing page isn't converting.

The problem isn't the copy. It's that the offer underneath the copy is weak — and no words fix a bad product.
```

```
Most marketers know exactly what their campaign is missing.

The problem isn't the creative. It's that nobody can articulate the one thing that makes this different from the competitor.
```

---

### 7. "Self Sabotage" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
Stop [specific marketing sabotage], [related action], then [rationalising it as strategy].

It's [specific cost this creates]:
```

**Examples:**
```
Stop writing "clever" copy that makes you sound smart.

It's ego serving over client results. And clients pay for results, not cleverness.
```

```
Stop outsourcing your email marketing before you've written one yourself.

It's delegation theatre. And it costs you understanding what actually works.
```

---

### 8. "Mistake You're Probably Making" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
If you [do specific marketing activity / at specific stage], you're probably making this mistake:

[Specific, non-obvious, painful mistake]
```

**Examples:**
```
If you're a freelance copywriter taking any client who pays, you're probably making this mistake:

Picking projects instead of packing your portfolio with work you'd be proud to show.
```

```
If you're a marketer at an early-stage startup, you're likely making this mistake:

Building "growth systems" before you have a offer that actually grows.
```

---

### 9. "Costly Mistakes" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
We made [X] mistakes in our first [Y] months/years.

([Specific cost in $, clients, lost revenue])
```

**Examples:**
```
We made 12 "quick wins" in our first quarter as an agency.

None of them brought recurring revenue. That's the problem — quick wins are just busy work in disguise.
```

```
I spent $40k on "premium placement" ads that converted at 0.3%.

The lesson: Placement only matters when your offer is strong enough to justify the premium.
```

---

### 10. "Shouldn't Ignore" — Marketing/Copywriting Version
```
Most [marketers / copywriters / agencies] don't talk about [uncomfortable marketing reality]:
```

**Examples:**
```
Most copywriters don't talk about the clients who made them worse at their job — and how they got out.
```

```
Most agencies don't talk about the pitches they won that became money pits. Only the ones that worked.
```
